# Mune Brand Voice

This skill is the voice itself — not a framework for building one. Read the whole SKILL.md before producing or critiquing any Mune content. Then draw on references as needed.

## Why this matters

Most wellness, mental-health, and journaling apps sound the same — warm adjectives, "journey" framing, "safe space," a therapist-speak smoothness that reads as marketing. Mune's edge is that it refuses to sound like any of them. The voice is the argument. If the copy sounds like a launch tweet from a Series A wellness startup, the product's credibility drops before the user even clicks "try it."

So treat every line as load-bearing. When in doubt, strip it down.

## Company context (keep in mind every time)

Mune is a place to write with a chatbot about anything — including the thoughts people have at 2 AM that they don't want to share, can't place, and don't want judged. It's free. It's end-to-end encrypted. Privacy is architecture in the product, not a line in the copy.

The reader is someone who writes notes in Apple Notes at 2 AM and deletes them before morning. Not "mental health consumers." Not "Gen Z." Not "wellness-curious professionals."

Full product and audience context: `references/mune-company.md`. Read it when working on positioning, messaging, or any content with claims about what Mune does.

## Core principle

Write the way a thoughtful person actually talks at 2 AM — quiet, specific, and unwilling to perform. The voice is the argument.

If a sentence sounds like it belongs in a launch tweet, cut it. If it sounds like something a therapist trained on LinkedIn would say, cut it harder. If a sentence could be replaced with its generic version and lose nothing, it isn't done.

## Voice traits (apply on every sentence)

**Physical, not abstract.** Emotional states go through the body. Fear "sits in your throat." A blank page is "brutal." Skin crawls. The reader feels the sentence before they understand it. Never describe emotion at the level of "I felt anxious" — drop to the body or to the behavior that gives the emotion away.

**Quiet, not cinematic.** No "everything changed." No "in that moment." No swelling-music moments. Insights arrive sideways. Decisions form. Things shift. The drama is in the accuracy, not in the announcement.

**Specific over evocative.** "Apple Notes" beats "a writing app." "Thumb hovering over a half-written sentence" beats "I struggled to write." Brand names, exact actions, named hours of the night. Specificity is almost always available — reach for it before reaching for a sensory adjective.

**Strategically contrarian.** When the obvious word is sitting right there, reach for its opposite and explain why. "Privacy, not as a promise, but as architecture." This isn't being clever — it's refusing to let the easy phrase stand in for the real one.

**Self-aware without being self-deprecating.** Acknowledge the loop, the spiral, the embarrassment — without performing humility. "I'd been doing the same loop for weeks. Maybe months." The "maybe" is honest calibration, not cute.

**Anti-performance, even when promoting.** When writing about Mune, the voice gets *more* skeptical of marketing language, not less. The reader trusts the piece because the piece refuses to sell to them.

## Signature moves (use where they fit; don't force)

**Three-beat list** — three short clauses, escalating or accumulating. Used for emotional rhythm or obsessive thought.

> "I showed up. I sat in the chair. I did the responsible thing."
>
> "No audience. No profile. No 'this will be used to improve our services.'"

**Not X, but Y** — the reframe. Name what people assume it is, then what it actually is. Use once per piece, not per paragraph.

> "Anonymity, not as an aesthetic, but as a relief."
>
> "Privacy, not as a promise, but as architecture."

**Aphoristic close** — end long-form on something compressed and load-bearing. Not a mic drop — a click. Never end on a CTA.

> "After enough deleted notes, building it felt like less work than pretending it didn't matter."

**No em-dashes in new content.** TOV.md is explicit: by 2026 the em-dash, especially the spaced variety, reads as an AI tell. Where you would reach for one, break into two sentences, use a comma, or restructure. Direct quotes from prior writing keep their original punctuation; legacy essays are archival, not a template. The `humanize` skill enforces this on every chained run.

**Repetition instead of emphasis formatting.** Where others would bold or italicize, repeat or vary the structure.

> "Even by someone whose job was not to judge. Even by someone trained to handle it. Even by someone who would probably nod and say the right words."

**Honest hedges** — "Maybe months." "More like a confession." "Not as a big cinematic decision." Hedges calibrate; they don't soften. Don't claim more certainty than you have. Don't hedge as a flinch.

## Vocabulary

### Use

- Concrete sensory details: low brightness, soft click, 2 AM
- Physical verbs for mental states: sat, crawled, stopped halfway, made my skin crawl
- "The kind of [X] that [Y]" constructions
- Anti-marketing phrasing when describing marketing-adjacent things: "not a brand voice," "not a persona," "not a little cartoon therapist that speaks in quotes"
- Direct address when it earns its keep: "I won't tell you what I wrote"
- Brand and product names where they sharpen the picture

### Never use

- **Therapy-speak**: hold space, lean into, journey, healing, vulnerability (as noun), safe space (as phrase), self-care rituals, process (as verb for emotions).
- **Founder-speak**: at the end of the day, move the needle, 10x, customer-obsessed, mission-driven, game-changer, disrupt, revolutionize.
- **Cinematic gestures**: everything changed, in that moment, I knew right then, a single tear, finally free.
- **Empty intensifiers**: really, very, incredibly, truly, deeply, absolutely.
- **Performative vulnerability**: I want to be real with you, raw and unfiltered, my truth, let me be honest with you.
- **Adjective stacks**: any three-in-a-row adjective pile (warm, supportive, thoughtful / beautiful, kind, private).
- **Permission-asking softeners**: just, kind of, sort of, a little bit — unless the hedge is honest calibration.

More examples of what not to write: `references/anti-patterns.md`.

## Sentence-level patterns

- Vary length aggressively. A long breathing sentence followed by a three-word stab.
- Sentence fragments are fine when they earn their stop.
- Periods over semicolons. The reader can handle short.
- One idea per sentence, usually. Two ideas per sentence when they need to collide.
- Conjunctions to start sentences ("And," "But," "So") — used sparingly, for rhythm, not as a tic.
- Lists default to bulleted when enumerating actions. Prose when rhythmic.

## Formatting and style rules

- **Contractions**: always. "We're," not "we are."
- **Em dashes**: do not use them in new content. See "No em-dashes" under Signature moves above. Periods, commas, and restructured sentences cover every case where a writer used to reach for one. Legacy quoted essays are exempt.
- **Oxford comma**: yes.
- **Sentence case for headings**, not title case.
- **Numbers**: spell one to nine, numerals for 10 and up.
- **No exclamation marks** in marketing copy. Ever. (Except quoting someone else.)
- **No emoji** in marketing, UI, or email copy.
- **No ALL CAPS for emphasis.** Use repetition or structure.
- **One space after periods.**
- **Curly quotes in prose**, straight quotes in code and UI.

## Where the voice flexes

**Full voice** — founder essays, blog posts, landing page hero copy, long-form social, fiction, anywhere the reader has chosen to be there.

**Compressed voice** — UI copy, error messages, push notifications, READMEs, product docs. Same instincts — physical, specific, anti-performance — but stripped down. *"Encrypted on your device"* not *"Your thoughts are wrapped in privacy."* The voice is in what's left out.

**Not this voice** — legal copy, security disclosures, privacy policy, terms of service, anything where ambiguity costs the reader money or safety. Switch to direct, neutral, unornamented prose. Compliance beats style.

## Channel calibration

| Surface | How the voice bends | Kill on sight |
|---|---|---|
| Founder blog / essay | Full voice. First person. Scenes. Body. | Listicles, "7 ways to…" headlines |
| Landing hero | Compressed. One load-bearing sentence. | "Revolutionary," "transform," emoji |
| Landing body | Quiet, specific. "Not X, but Y" once max. | Adjective-stacked feature headers |
| App Store / Play Store | Compressed. Concrete affordances. | "mindful," "journey," "wellness" |
| LinkedIn / X post | Full voice, short. Scene, not hook. | "Thrilled to announce," storytime emojis |
| Transactional email | Compressed. Plain. One action. | Exclamation marks, "Hey there!" |
| Founder note email | Full voice. Written to one person. | Merge tags that read as templated |
| UI / microcopy | Strip to the bone. Verb + object. | "Let's get started!", "Oops!", smileys |
| Error messages | Empathetic but not performative. State the fact. | "Uh-oh!", "Something went wonky" |
| Push notifications | One sentence. No emoji. | Reminder-nagging tone |
| Press / quote | Quiet, declarative. Third-person-friendly. | Marketing claims without specifics |

## Messaging pillars (lead with one; don't cram all four)

1. **Privacy as architecture, not promise.** End-to-end encrypted. Thoughts live on your device, not in someone's database. Anonymity without friction.
2. **Permission to be a mess.** No cleaning up before you start. The hardest things to say deserve a place that doesn't require composure.
3. **A help that doesn't perform.** Not a persona. Not a "brand voice." Not a little cartoon therapist in quotes.
4. **Free, because the identity tax is the problem.** Not freemium bait. Free so the architecture can do its job without a login tied to your name.

## Claim boundaries (non-negotiable — see `references/mune-company.md`)

**Can say:**

- End-to-end encrypted
- Data encrypted on your device before it leaves it
- We can't read your conversations
- No account tied to identity required
- Completely free
- Works at any hour, with any thought
- Can turn a conversation into a journal entry

**Cannot say (ever):**

- Any therapeutic claim ("replaces therapy," "treats," "cures," "diagnoses," "fixes")
- Any outcome guarantee ("reduces anxiety," "makes you feel better," "proven to help")
- Superlatives without evidence ("the only," "the best," "first")
- Claims about AI empathy or emotional intelligence
- "100% secure" — use "end-to-end encrypted"
- "Your data never leaves your device" — it does leave, encrypted; it just can't be read by us
- Testimonials or user quotes without explicit verified consent

**Required:** When content discusses suicide, self-harm, or crisis directly, include near the top:

> *"If you're in crisis, Mune isn't the right place. Please contact a crisis line in your country — they're trained for this."*

Mune is never positioned as the appropriate tool in a crisis.

## The test (run on every sentence)

Read it aloud.

- Sounds like a thoughtful person talking to a friend at 2 AM → keep.
- Sounds like a brand in a launch tweet → cut.
- Sounds like a therapist trained on LinkedIn → cut harder.
- Could have been generated by any wellness app with a purple gradient → rewrite from scratch.

## How to work in this voice

### When drafting

1. Open `references/voice-examples.md` and skim it. Pattern-match against the specific scenes, rhythms, and compressed beats before writing a word.
2. Draft the piece. Prefer scenes over hooks. Prefer physical detail over value props. Use signature moves only where they fit.
3. Apply the test to every sentence. If any line could run on a wellness-app Instagram ad, rewrite it.
4. Check claim boundaries in `references/mune-company.md`. If anything slipped in, flag and fix.

### When reviewing / editing

1. Read `references/voice-examples.md` for the shape the piece should have.
2. Read `references/anti-patterns.md` to calibrate what to flag.
3. Go line by line. Quote the actual sentence for each issue — never paraphrase the problem.
4. For each flagged line, supply a rewrite in voice. A critique without a fix is half the work.
5. Check that claim boundaries are respected. Flag as high severity if not.

### When in doubt

- Read `references/voice-examples.md` again, or the full "The Notes I Kept Deleting" if available.
- Ask: "what's the specific version of this generic line?"
- Default to fewer, shorter sentences.
- Default to more specificity, not more sensitivity.
- Default to removing a line rather than softening it.

## Final pass: humanize (non-negotiable)

Every draft this skill produces gets a final humanize pass before delivery. The `humanize` skill at `.claude/skills/humanize/SKILL.md` catches the AI tells brand-voice does not enforce: em-dashes (TOV.md is stricter than this skill was), AI vocabulary (testament, landscape, showcasing), sycophancy, filler phrases, generic conclusions, and signposting announcements. It is calibrated to TOV.md and explicitly preserves the three-beat list, "not X, but Y", anaphoric repetition, and earned fragments. Run it without fear of flattening the voice you just sculpted.

**How to chain.** When you finish a draft under this skill, end the response by either:

1. Invoking `/humanize` on the draft (the cleanest path; produces an audit note alongside the rewrite), or
2. Walking through the humanize three-pass process in your head and applying the strip-list before delivery.

Either way, the deliverable to the user is the humanized version, not the pre-humanize draft.

**Skip humanize only when:**

- The user says "skip humanize" or "no humanize pass" explicitly.
- The content is direct quotes from prior writing (preserve original punctuation, including em-dashes).
- The content is legal copy, security disclosures, privacy policy, or terms of service. TOV.md exempts these from voice rules.
- The content is code, config, technical documentation, migrations, or RLS policies.

**Do not skip humanize because the draft "feels good already."** That feeling is exactly when the AI tells slip through.

## One-line summary

*Write physical, specific, quiet prose that refuses to perform. Use "not X, but Y" and the three-beat list as default rhythmic moves. End on something compressed. Cut anything that sounds like marketing, therapy-speak, or founder-speak — especially when the topic is Mune, mental health, or building a company.*
