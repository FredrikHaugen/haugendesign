# Preserve list: Mune signature moves

These are the moves the off-the-shelf humanizer would flag as AI tells but that TOV.md uses deliberately. When humanize encounters one of these in a draft, it must leave the passage alone. Stripping a signature move is worse than leaving an AI tell, because it flattens Mune's voice toward generic-thoughtful-person, which is the failure mode the brand voice was built to escape.

## 1. Three-beat list

Three short clauses, escalating or accumulating. Used for emotional rhythm or obsessive thought. The third beat usually compresses or surprises.

**Preserve these:**

> "I showed up. I sat in the chair. I did the responsible thing."

> "No audience. No profile. No 'this will be used to improve our services.'"

> "Anonymity, not as an aesthetic, but as a relief. Privacy, not as a promise, but as architecture. Thoughtful support, without the persona."

**Why this is a signature, not a tell:** the beats are concrete, escalating, and load-bearing. Each clause adds new information. Replacing the triad with a single sentence loses meaning.

**Generic version that humanize SHOULD strip:**

> "Innovation, inspiration, and insights." (adjective triad, no specificity)

> "Faster, smarter, better." (filler comparatives)

> "Mind, body, and soul." (cliché triad)

How to tell: if you can swap any two beats and lose nothing, it is generic. If the order matters and the third beat compresses, it is a Mune three-beat list.

## 2. "Not X, but Y" reframes

The reframe move. Name what people assume, then name what it actually is. TOV.md says: use once per piece, not per paragraph.

**Preserve these:**

> "Anonymity, not as an aesthetic, but as a relief."

> "Privacy, not as a promise, but as architecture."

> "Free, not as bait, but because the identity tax is the problem."

**Why this is a signature, not a tell:** the X and Y reframe a real tension a thoughtful reader has felt. The Y earns the X by being more accurate, not just clever.

**Generic version humanize SHOULD strip:**

> "Not just a tool, but a partner." (SaaS framing, no real tension)

> "It's not just X, it's Y." (unspecific X, vague Y)

> "Not your average journaling app, but a sanctuary." (collapses into cliché)

How to tell: replace the X and Y with their generic versions. If the sentence still says something, it is a real reframe. If it dissolves into vagueness, it is generic.

## 3. Anaphoric repetition

Where other writers would bold or italicize, TOV.md repeats. Three-clause anaphora is the standard form.

**Preserve these:**

> "Even by someone whose job was not to judge. Even by someone trained to handle it. Even by someone who would probably nod and say the right words."

> "Not the brand, not the platform, not the seven-step framework, just a place to think."

**Why this is a signature, not a tell:** repetition accumulates evidence. Each repeated clause tightens the case.

**Generic version humanize SHOULD strip:**

> "Innovation. Innovation. Innovation." (emphasis as substitute for content)

> "Mune is private. Mune is encrypted. Mune is yours." (declarative-style branding without progression)

How to tell: Mune's anaphora compounds; generic anaphora declares. If each repeated clause sharpens the previous one, preserve. If they are interchangeable, strip.

## 4. Earned sentence fragments

TOV.md: "Sentence fragments are fine when they earn their stop." A fragment earns its stop when adding a subject or verb would dilute the punch.

**Preserve these:**

> "Scene, not hook."

> "More like a confession."

> "Maybe months."

> "Quiet, not cinematic."

**Why this is a signature, not a tell:** the fragment lands harder than the full sentence would. Forcing it into a complete sentence ("It was more like a confession") softens it.

**Generic version humanize SHOULD strip:**

> "Game-changer." (laziness)

> "Mind. Blown." (meme energy)

> "So much value." (hollow fragment)

How to tell: a Mune fragment compresses meaning. A generic fragment substitutes attitude for meaning.

## 5. Honest hedges

TOV.md: "Hedges calibrate; they don't soften. Don't claim more certainty than you have. Don't hedge as a flinch."

**Preserve these:**

> "Maybe months."

> "More like a confession that kept stopping halfway."

> "Not as a big cinematic decision."

> "Probably trained to handle it."

**Why this is a signature, not a tell:** the hedge admits to a real uncertainty about a specific claim. It increases credibility, not decreases it.

**Generic version humanize SHOULD strip:**

> "Could potentially possibly help." (hedging the claim into nothing)

> "Some users may find it useful." (CYA hedge)

> "Generally speaking, in many cases, often." (stacked hedges)

How to tell: a Mune hedge calibrates a specific number, scene, or claim. A generic hedge weakens the entire sentence to avoid commitment.

## 6. Aphoristic close

Long-form ends on one compressed line. Not a mic drop, a click. Never a CTA.

**Preserve these:**

> "After enough deleted notes, building it felt like less work than pretending it didn't matter."

> "Some thoughts only stay yours if no one is listening."

**Why this is a signature, not a tell:** the close compresses the whole piece into one load-bearing sentence. It depends on what came before.

**Generic version humanize SHOULD strip:**

> "The future of journaling is here." (CTA close)

> "Try Mune today." (CTA close)

> "Exciting times ahead." (generic conclusion)

> "It's a journey, not a destination." (cliché close)

How to tell: a Mune close earns its compression by sitting on top of specific scenes and arguments. A generic close could end any piece on any topic.

## 7. Em-dashes in legacy or quoted material

TOV.md prohibits em-dashes in new content but explicitly preserves them in direct quotes from prior writing.

**Preserve these:**

> Original line in "The Notes I Kept Deleting": "It was somewhere around 2 AM – phone on low brightness, thumb hovering over a half-written sentence in a notes app."

If a draft quotes legacy Fredrik essays, leave the em-dashes alone. They are archival voice.

**Strip these:**

Any em-dash introduced in new copy, especially:

> "Mune is the place — quiet, private, yours — where the hardest thoughts can land."

That em-dash pair is exactly the AI tell TOV.md flags. Replace with periods or commas.

How to tell: is the em-dash in a quote attributed to prior published Fredrik writing? Preserve. Otherwise, strip.

## Edge cases and judgment calls

**Two beats, not three.** If a passage uses two clauses where three is the signature, ask: was it deliberate or accidental? If the second clause carries real weight, leave it. If it feels truncated, flag for human review rather than stripping or extending.

**A three-beat list with a weak third beat.** If beats one and two are concrete and beat three is generic ("a journey, a destination, a way of life"), do NOT silently strip. Flag for human review with the line quoted, so Fredrik can decide whether to rewrite the third beat or kill the list.

**Anaphora in legal copy.** TOV.md exempts legal copy from voice rules. Anaphora that appears in privacy policy or terms of service is probably accidental and not a signature move. Treat it case by case but lean toward leaving it.

**"Not X, but Y" used twice in one piece.** TOV.md says once per piece, max. If a draft uses it twice, flag the weaker instance for human review. Do not silently strip.

## One-line summary

If you remove one of these moves, you are sanding off Mune's voice. If you flag it for review, you are doing the job. When in doubt, flag, do not edit.
